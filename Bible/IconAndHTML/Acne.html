Acne

ICD-9: 706.1

ICD-10: L70.0

Epidemiology

Age of Onset

  • Affects ~ 85% of young people.
  • Most commonly begins in puberty, but may appear first at 25 years or older.

Sex

  • More severe in men than in women

Ethnic or Geographic Variation

  • Lower incidence in Asians and Africans.

Triggers and Exacerbating Factors

  • Acnegenic mineral oils, rarely dioxin and others
  • Drugs, including lithium, hydantoin, isoniazid, glucocorticoids, oral contraceptives, iodides, bromides and androgens (e.g., testosterone), danazol.
  • Emotional stress
  • Occlusion and pressure on the skin, such as by leaning face on hands (acne mechanica).
  • No food or type of food, including chocolate or fatty foods, cause acne/

Pathogenesis

  • Change in the keratinization pattern of pilosebaceous unit
  • keratinous material becomes more dense, blocks sebum and forms comedones (keratin plugs)
  • Linoleic acid is decreased, causing an increase in keratinocytes.
  • Comedones, androgens, and bacteria interaction leads to inflammation

Clinical Manifestations

History

  • Lesions last weeks to months and are often worse in fall and winter.

Skin Lesions

  • Comedones—open (blackheads) or closed (whiteheads); comedonal acne
  • Papules and papulopustules—i.e., a papule topped by a pustule; papulopustular acne
  • Nodules or cysts—1–4 cm in diameter; nodulocystic acne.
  • Soft nodules result from repeated ruptures and reencapsulation with inflammation, abscess formation, and foreign-body reaction.
  • Cysts are actually pseudocysts as they are not lined by epithelium but represent fluctuating abscesses
  • Round isolated single nodules and cysts coalesce to linear mounds and sinus tracts
  • Scars: atrophic depressed (often pitted) or hypertrophic (at times, keloidal).
  • Seborrhea of the face and scalp often present and sometimes severe.

Laboratory Examination

  • No laboratory examinations required.

Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis

  • Comedones are required for diagnosis of any type of acne.
  • Face: S. aureus folliculitis, pseudofolliculitis barbae, rosacea, perioral dermatitis.
  • Trunk: Malassezia folliculitis, "hot-tub" pseudomonas folliculitis, S. aureus folliculitis, and acne-like conditions (see above).

Course

  • Most often clears spontaneously by the early twenties
  • Can persist to the fourth decade or older.
  • Flares occur in the winter and with the onset of menses.
  • Scarring will occur without proper treatment

Management

  • Include psychological impact of acne for each patient in therapeutic plan
  • Remove plugging of pilar drainage, reduce sebum production, and treat bacterial colonization.

Mild Acne

  • Topical antibiotics (clindamycin and erythromycin), applied in daytime
  • Benzoyl peroxide gels (2%, 5%, or 10%), applied in daytime
  • Topical retinoids (tretinoin, adapalene) gradually increasing concentration from 0.01% to 0.025% to 0.05% cream/gel or liquid, applied in evening
  • After improvement, medication is reduced to the lowest effective maintenance.
  • Improvement occurs over 2-5 months but may take even longer for noninflamed comedones.
  • Combination therapy is best, using benzoyl peroxide–erythromycin gels plus topical retinoids (tretinoin or tazarotene gel, adapalene).

Moderate Acne

  • Add oral antibiotics to mild acne regimen above: minocycline, 50–100 mg twice daily, or doxycycline, 50–100 mg twice daily, tapered to 50- mg/d as acne lessens.
  • For girls and women, oral estrogens combined with progesterone or antiandrogens prevent outbreaks though they will recur if treatment is stopped.
  • Cerebrovascular accidents are a serious risk.
  • For inflammatory cysts and nodules, use intralesional triamcinolone (0.05 mL of a 3- to 5- mg/mL suspension).

Severe Acne

  • Add systemic treatment with isotretinoin for cystic or conglobate acne or if above treatments do not resolve outbreaks.
  • Dosage: Isotretinoin, 0.5 to 1 mg/kg given in divided doses with food; up to 2 mg/kg
  • Isotretinoin is teratogenic. Therefore, pregnancy must be prevented and effective contraception is necessary, i.e., oral.
  • Do not use tetracycline and isotretinoin togetehr as both may cause pseudotumor cerebri.
  • Determine blood lipids and transaminases (ALT, AST) before therapy to assess cardiovascular risks.
  • When levels of serum triglycerides rise above 800 mg/L, acute pancreatitis may occur.
  • Patients should not take vitamin A.
  • Night blindness has been reported, and patients should be warned about driving at night.
  • Patients may have decreased tolerance to contact lenses during and after therapy.
  • Eczema-like rash due to drug-induced dryness often appears, and this responds dramatically to low potency (class III) topical glucocorticoids.
  • Dry lips and cheilitis occur in practically all patients and must be treated.
  • Reversible thinning of hair may occur very rarely, as may paronychia.
  • Dryness of nasal mucosa and nose bleeds (rare).
  • Rarely, depression, headaches, arthritis, and muscular pain.
  • Systemic glucocorticoids.
  • Intralesional triamcinolone (0.05 mL of a 3 to 5 mg/mL solution).
